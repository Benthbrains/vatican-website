Welcome to Vatican City – the smallest independent state in the world by both area and population, covering just 44 hectares (110 acres) and home to approximately 800 residents. Encircled entirely by Rome, Italy, it serves as the spiritual and administrative center of the Roman Catholic Church, which boasts over 1.3 billion members worldwide. Governed as an absolute elective monarchy, the Pope holds supreme legislative, executive, and judicial authority. Established in 1929 via the Lateran Treaty, Vatican City is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a hub of unparalleled artistic, historical, and religious treasures.

St. Peter's Basilica

The largest church in the world, built over the tomb of St. Peter, with a capacity for 60,000 worshippers. It features Michelangelo's Pietà and Bernini's bronze baldachin.

Sistine Chapel

Commissioned by Pope Sixtus IV in 1473, renowned for Michelangelo's ceiling frescoes depicting the Book of Genesis and his monumental The Last Judgment on the altar wall.

Vatican Museums

Founded in the 16th century, housing over 70,000 works of art across 54 galleries, including ancient Egyptian artifacts, Renaissance masterpieces, and the renowned Gallery of Maps.

Vatican City issues its own euros, passports, and postage stamps, and maintains a unique Swiss Guard for papal protection since 1506.

Historical Timeline

From its ancient origins as a pilgrimage site to its modern status as a sovereign entity, the history of Vatican City is intertwined with the papacy and the evolution of Christianity.

1st Century AD

St. Peter, the first Pope, is martyred and buried on Vatican Hill, establishing it as a sacred site for early Christians under Roman persecution.

4th Century

Emperor Constantine the Great legalizes Christianity and constructs the original St. Peter's Basilica over St. Peter's tomb, marking the site's transformation into a major basilica.

1506–1626

The current Renaissance St. Peter's Basilica is rebuilt under Popes Julius II and others, involving architects like Bramante, Raphael, and Michelangelo, who designed the iconic dome.

1870

The Capture of Rome ends the Papal States; Popes become 'Prisoners of the Vatican' until resolution with the Italian government.

1929

The Lateran Treaty, signed by Pope Pius XI and Benito Mussolini, creates Vatican City as an independent sovereign state, resolving the 'Roman Question'.

1984

UNESCO designates the Historic Centre of Rome, including Vatican City, as a World Heritage Site for its profound cultural and religious significance.

2025

Pope Francis passes; the conclave elects Robert Francis Cardinal Prevost as Pope Leo XIV, the first American pontiff, ushering in a new era focused on social justice and AI ethics.

Key Attractions

Vatican City is a treasure trove of art, architecture, and spirituality. Explore its iconic landmarks, each telling a story of faith and human ingenuity.

St. Peter's Square

Designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini between 1656 and 1667, this elliptical piazza accommodates 400,000 people and features 140 saints atop its colonnades, symbolizing the Church's embrace.

The Dome of St. Peter's

Michelangelo's 136-meter masterpiece offers breathtaking views from its lantern; ascend 551 steps or elevator for a panorama of Rome, with the obelisk below marking ancient Egyptian origins.

Vatican Gardens

Spanning 23 hectares with manicured lawns, fountains, and the Lourdes Grotto replica, these gardens provide a serene escape, accessible via guided tours revealing papal history.

Vatican Apostolic Library

One of the oldest libraries globally, founded in 1475, preserving 75,000 codices including the Codex Vaticanus, the earliest complete Bible manuscript from the 4th century.

Swiss Guard

Founded in 1506, this elite corps of 135 soldiers in Renaissance-inspired uniforms protects the Pope; witness the ceremonial changing of the guard in St. Peter's Square.

Raphael Rooms

Four frescoed chambers in the Apostolic Palace, painted by Raphael and pupils from 1508–1524, depicting theology, philosophy, and papal history in exquisite Renaissance style.

The Holy Father

Pope Leo XIV

Bishop of Rome • Vicar of Christ • Successor of St. Peter • 267th Pope

Born Robert Francis Prevost on September 14, 1955, in Chicago, Illinois, USA. Elected May 8, 2025, as the first American Pope, marking a historic milestone for the Church.

Early Life and Ministry

  • Ordained a priest of the Order of Saint Augustine (OSA) in 1982 after studying in Chicago and Rome.
  • Served as a missionary in Peru for over two decades, becoming fluent in Spanish and dedicating himself to pastoral care among the poor.
  • Appointed Bishop of Chiclayo, Peru, in 2015, and elevated to Cardinal in 2023 by Pope Francis.
  • Prior to his election, served as Prefect of the Dicastery for Bishops, influencing global episcopal appointments.

Papal Election and Vision

  • Chosen on the fourth ballot during the 2025 conclave following the death of Pope Francis, with white smoke signaling his election at 18:07 CEST.
  • Selected the name Leo XIV to honor Pope Leo XIII's social teachings in Rerum Novarum (1891), adapting them to modern challenges like AI, workers' rights, and climate justice.
  • In his first address, emphasized synodality, ecumenism, and the Church as a 'beacon of light' amid global 'dark nights' of conflict and division.
  • Canonized seven new saints on October 19, 2025, during the Jubilee Year, highlighting diverse global holiness from Asia, Africa, and the Americas.
"In this era of rapid technological change, let us echo Leo XIII's call for justice: technology must serve humanity, not enslave it. The Church stands as a guardian of dignity in the face of artificial intelligence and the new industrial revolution."
— Pope Leo XIV, Address to the College of Cardinals, May 10, 2025
As the first U.S.-born Pope, Leo XIV brings a unique perspective on global issues, including peace efforts in Ukraine, the Middle East, and interfaith dialogue.

Advice for Booking

Visiting Vatican City requires preparation to fully appreciate its wonders. Advance booking is essential, especially during high seasons and papal events.

Tickets & Booking

Secure timed-entry tickets for Vatican Museums (€17–€33) and Sistine Chapel online. St. Peter's Basilica is free, but dome access costs €8–€10. Official site: museivaticani.va. Guided tours available for deeper insights.

Opening Hours

Vatican Museums: Mon–Sat 8:00–19:00 (last entry 17:00), Sun (except last) 9:00–18:00.
St. Peter's Basilica: Daily 7:00–19:00 (Apr–Sep), 7:00–18:30 (Oct–Mar).
Gardens Tours: Tue–Sun, book in advance.

Dress Code & Etiquette

Modest attire required: shoulders and knees covered for both men and women. No hats inside churches, and silence is observed in sacred spaces. Photography allowed except in Sistine Chapel.

Getting There

Metro Line A to Ottaviano or Lepanto stations (10-min walk). Free entry via security at St. Peter's Square. Expect queues; arrive early for papal audiences on Wednesdays.

Practical Tips

Currency: Euro (€). Free Wi-Fi in museums. Water fountains available. Download the Vatican News app for live papal events. Avoid peak summer heat; visit in spring or fall.

Pro Tip: Join a papal audience for €25–€50; tickets via Prefecture of the Papal Household.

Accessibility

Wheelchair access available; request in advance. Elevators in museums and basilica. Audio guides in multiple languages for hearing-impaired visitors.
